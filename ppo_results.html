Conclusions

Primary Finding

The source of randomness (PRNG vs QRNG) does not produce statistically significant differences in either maze structural properties or reinforcement learning agent generalization performance.

  • Maze Structure: All 9 structural metrics (shortest path, tortuosity, dead ends, junctions, corridors, turns) showed identical medians between PRNG and QRNG mazes. Mann-Whitney U tests confirmed no significant difference (0/9 metrics, all p > 0.05, lowest p = 0.125).
  • Agent Performance: Both PRNG-trained and QRNG-trained agents achieved comparable success rates on evaluation (PRNG: 78% intra / 76% cross; QRNG: 79% intra / 68% cross). The QRNG agent showed a slightly larger generalization gap (11% vs 2%), but both agents demonstrated cross-distribution competence.
  • Generalization: The PRNG agent generalized slightly better across maze types (2% gap vs 11% gap), though both agents performed well on unseen mazes from either distribution.
  • Training Convergence: The PRNG agent reached a higher final success rate (94% vs 90%) and saw far more unique mazes (3,352 vs 144), suggesting the seed diversity during training may matter more than the randomness source.
  • Implication: For DFS-based maze generation on a 20×20 grid, PRNG seeds produce statistically equivalent maze distributions to QRNG seeds. Quantum randomness does not confer a measurable advantage for training RL maze navigation agents.
